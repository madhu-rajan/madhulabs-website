Option A

Full NE Rotation

The mandap is repositioned to the southwest corner of the terrace. The couple faces northeast — the Ishanya direction, considered the most auspicious in Vastu Shastra. Seating is angled on a diagonal to face the mandap. The baraat enters from the east and processes diagonally toward the couple. This requires a significant layout change.

Pros

  • Couple faces Northeast (Ishanya) — the most auspicious direction per Vastu Shastra
  • Vineyard view is partially behind the couple — still in the photo backdrop
  • May 29 sunset (NW) — late sunset means ceremony likely in full daylight, no harsh low-angle glare
  • Diagonal layout feels intimate and unique

Cons

  • Diagonal seating is less space-efficient — may lose some seats or require tighter row spacing
  • Baraat processional is diagonal, not a straight aisle — harder to choreograph
  • More complex layout for the venue/design team to execute
  • The west lounge furniture must be fully removed to accommodate mandap

Changes Required

  • Move mandap to the southwest corner of the terrace
  • Rotate and angle all seating ~45 degrees to face southwest
  • Remove the west Amaterra outdoor furniture entirely
  • Keep east lounge furniture — functions as pre-ceremony / baraat staging
  • Reposition swing to east side (remains a photo feature)
  • Create a diagonal aisle from the east entry to the mandap

Key Considerations

  • Aisle width should be 5-6 ft minimum for processions
  • Sound/music setup will need to account for the angled orientation

Sun & Lighting Analysis

May 29, 2026 — Portland, OR

Sunset time: ~8:50 PM PDT

Sunset direction: Northwest (approx. 300 degrees azimuth)

Sun angle: High in the sky through late afternoon — golden hour begins around 7:30 PM

Key takeaway: A daytime ceremony (before 6 PM) will have bright, overhead light — no low-angle glare for anyone. Evening ceremonies get beautiful golden-hour light.

Recommendation

★ Option B — NE-Angled Mandap (Minimal Change)

Option B is the clear winner for practicality. It achieves the auspicious NE-facing direction for the couple with zero changes to the floor plan. The vineyard backdrop is preserved, all 120 seats remain, furniture stays in place, and there is no venue coordination or redesign required. The vadyaar simply angles the ceremonial setup within the existing mandap.

The only trade-off is that guests on the far west side will see slightly more of the couple's profile rather than a direct face-on view. This is a minor visual difference that most guests won't notice, and the photographer can easily adjust for it.

Option A is the right choice only if the vadyaar specifically requires the entire ceremony space (guests, aisle, and all) to be oriented NE — not just the couple. Confirm this with the vadyaar before committing to the major layout change.
