Mukund & Divya · May 29, 2026

A Parents’ Speech

Madhu & Akhila

Short version · ~14 min

AKHILA

Good evening, Namaskara, and Vanakkam everyone. Thank you all for being here and for surrounding Mukund and Divya with so much love on this beautiful day. Your presence means the world to us.

MADHU

And thank you for showing up for what, after 26 years of trying, is finally our chance to share a few stories about Mukund in front of every single person he knows. Akhilu and I have been waiting a long time for this!

Let me start with some numbers. Mukund was born on May 1st, 2000, at 1:23 AM. One, two, three. The boy showed up in numerical order. He has been precise about everything since.

Akhilu and I named him Mukund — one of the names of Lord Krishna. Small bit of family history: I thought it would be nice for all our names to start with M. Akhilu then did all the hard work narrowing down the names that matched. So I think I deserve at least 50% credit — for giving the idea.

AKHILA

I'll give you that one, Chinnu.

MADHU

Thank you, Chinnu.

The name Mukund means "the one who grants liberation." In the original story, Lord Krishna's birth was a midnight cosmic drama — storm, serpent, the river itself parting to let his father carry him through.

Our version was less theatrical. In the early morning hours of May 1st, a very small baby with a giant head — taken after me, naturally — announced himself to the world at the top of his lungs, as if to say, "Excuse me, why did you bring me out here? I was perfectly happy inside Amma. Send me back, please."

But he has spent every year since living up to the liberation part of the name — mostly by trying to liberate himself from us. - - - AKHILA: Even as a little boy, Mukund stood out for his curiosity, his resilience, and his patience. The very first toy he reached for was a Lego block — which, honestly, explained a lot. He loved building things, organizing things, fixing things. A real-life Bob the Builder with a sparkle in his eyes.

MADHU

And Chinnu — you remember what he did at the state Lego championship.

AKHILA

Oh, that I remember.

MADHU

Mukund led his FLL Lego team to win the Oregon state championship — beating 120 other teams. And he did it by redesigning their robot from scratch with a four-wheel drive design no team had attempted before.

AKHILA

That was the moment we realized he was not just playing with Legos. He was an engineer. - - - AKHILA: And he was independent from day one literally. Madhu, remember ? —

MADHU

Tell them, Chinnu.

AKHILA

We had a little bike with training wheels, and the moment Mukund realized Madhu was not holding on anymore, he just dropped the bike and ran. No goodbye. No thank you. Just freedom.

But that same fiercely independent little boy, on his very first day of preschool, cried so hard and held on to me so tightly that the teacher had to carry him on her lap for the entire class. That is our Mukund — fiercely independent, and deeply attached to the people he loves.

And from the day Medini was born four years later, the two of them were inseparable. Best friends, teammates, and occasionally partners in crime. They still are. - - - AKHILA: There were many funny stories along the way.

MADHU

Many.

AKHILA

There was the time he ate an entire container of sweet homeopathy pills, and when we asked what happened, he innocently said, "Amma, I didn't do anything. I just swallowed all of them."

MADHU

And there was the shaving incident in Bangalore. Mukund was about 7. He used to love watching me shave —

AKHILA

One morning, we heard crying from the bathroom. We rushed in. He was standing there with Madhu's razor, with a cut on his lip. And he looked up at us, with the most innocent face, and said —

BOTH

"A mosquito bit me!"

AKHILA

And then there was the Game Boy. He came to me holding it. "Amma, it is not working." I asked what happened. He said, "It felt dirty. So I put water on it to clean it."

MADHU

That was the end of the Game Boy. But the start of a beautiful engineering career.

AKHILA

Take it from here, Chinnu.

MADHU

Happily. The boy who once washed his own Game Boy to clean it now works as a software engineer at Meta — Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, the entire empire. The same kid who short-circuited his own Game Boy is now writing code that runs on three billion phones. Okay — slight exaggeration. But you get the idea.

To be fair — Akhilu has held a no-phones-at-dinner rule in our house for years. We even had a stretch where, every night at the table, we each shared the highlight and lowlight of our day. That part — I would like to point out — was my idea. But now our son works at Meta, and he is professionally undermining all of it, one notification at a time.

AKHILA

Every notification. - - - MADHU: Mukund is also a perfect demonstration of how genetics work. He got Akhilu's eyes — beautiful. He got her cleanliness, her meticulousness, her fastidious nature. From me, he inherited my big nose with a dent and my terrible short-term memory. So Akhila clearly won that round. My one positive contribution to this entire project remains: the letter M. - - - MADHU: We have always been an outdoors family. And there is one day with Mukund I will never forget — when you, Medini, and I scaled Mount St. Helens together. We trained for months. And on the day, the three of us made it to the top together. I will carry that day with me forever. And buddy — we still have Mount Adams on the list.

And Mukund — I am so proud that you recently ran a half marathon. You are now in the exclusive M club with me. I take credit for that one too. - - - AKHILA: Some memories stay with a mother forever. I will always remember the night Mukund called me at 11 PM, when he was a teenager preparing for a speech competition after a difficult day. He simply said, "Amma, I just wanted to talk." And then there were our quiet walks together in the beautiful Pacific Northwest, just the two of us, surprise visits for my birthday, every handwritten note, taking our whole family to watch the Nutcracker— moments I will treasure for the rest of my life.

From Portland to Chicago to Seattle, Mukund has carried himself with quiet strength and grace through every transition. He has developed friendships,has thrived, and has grown into the thoughtful, grounded man we are so proud of today. And through all of it — he has remained a devoted foodie. Nothing makes me happier than cooking his favorite meals. - - - AKHILA: Divya — today we welcome you with open hearts into our family. Into our circle of warmth, love, laughter, and deep care for one another. You bring so much joy to Mukund's life, and it is easy to see why he cherishes you so deeply.

Mukund and Divya — marriage will bring beautiful seasons and difficult ones. It is not about chasing perfection. It is about kindness. Patience. Forgiveness. Respect. And choosing each other — over and over again.

May you always remain each other's safest place. - - - MADHU: Before we close, Akhila and I want to thank a few very important people who have made this day what it is.

AKHILA

First, to my beloved sister Anitha — I would be lost without you. Thank you for being my rock, for traveling all the way from Bangalore, and for that beautiful song.

To my lovely sister-in-law Veena — I appreciate you more than words can ever express.

To our dear friends — Vasanthi and Vasu, Malini and Arathi in Bengaluru; Padmashree and Seshu, Veena and Jayadev, Shanmu and Arun, Ruchi and Rahul, Sudha and Bhaskar, Anjana and Vijay here in Portland; and to Madhu's cousins Savitha, Kavitha, and Raghu-Soumya — your love and support have meant the world to us.

MADHU

And from my side — to Ravi, my brother-in-law — thank you for keeping things light among our families. Remember the time you convinced a toll booth attendant that I was your spoiled son who had partied away all our money? And he believed every word of it.

To Rhea, my favorite niece and a sharp software engineer; Chintu, my favorite nephew and a budding entrepreneur; and my cousins Smitha and Ramya and their kids — thank you for making the trip.

To Omesh, my friend from the CG CoreEL days in Pune, here today with your son — thank you for coming.

And to Venkatesh, my closest cousin — more like a brother than a cousin. He could not be here due to visa restrictions. Venkatesh — you are missed today more than anyone.

BOTH

To all of our family and friends back in India, and to all of our friends here in Portland who are too many to name — thank you for being part of our lives. Your blessings and love have carried us here. - - - AKHILA: And to my beloved daughter Medini — you warm my heart every day by always being there for me, without judgment. Thank you, baby girl!

To my beloved husband of thirty years, Madhu — thank you for being my best and forever friend.

MADHU

Akhilu — I have spent this entire speech taking credit for the letter M and giving you credit for everything else, and honestly, that math is about right. You gave Mukund your eyes, your patience, your meticulousness, your discipline, and your kindness. You raised these two extraordinary kids.

Mukund and Divya — exactly thirty years ago, Akhilu and I started our own version of what you are starting today. - - - AKHILA: Today is not only the union of Mukund and Divya, but the coming together of two families and two traditions — Kannada and Tamil — joined by shared values of love, faith, generosity, and community.

To Jey and Amudha — Divya's wonderful parents — and to her sister Sneha: today is the day our two families become one. You are ours now, for life. Madhu and I look forward to a lifetime ahead — many celebrations, many holidays, and many quiet evenings together.

MADHU

We are so glad to call you family.

Unwavering faith in God has always been the bedrock of our lives. As you begin this new chapter, we pray that same faith continues to guide and strengthen you both. May your home be filled with peace, health, prosperity, love, laughter, shared dreams, and deep companionship. - - - MADHU: Mukund & Divya — we love you. Divya — welcome to the chaos. You got the eyes, the meticulousness, the engineer's brain, and a husband who will figure out how to fix the car before he tells you it is broken. You are going to be just fine.

BOTH

To Mukund and Divya — cheers.
