Mukund & Divya · May 29, 2026

A Parents’ Speech

Madhu & Akhila

AKHILA

Good evening, Namaskara, and Vanakkam everyone. Thank you all for being here and for surrounding Mukund and Divya with so much love on this beautiful day. Your presence means the world to us.

MADHU

And thank you for showing up for what, after 26 years of trying, is finally my chance to embarrass Mukund in front of every single person he knows. Akhilu and I have been waiting a long time for this.

Let me start with some numbers. Mukund was born on May 1st, 2000, at 1:23 AM. One, two, three. The boy showed up in numerical order. He has been precise about everything since.

Akhilu and I named him Mukund — one of the names of Lord Krishna. Small bit of family history I want on the record tonight: my entire contribution to naming our firstborn was the requirement that his name start with M — because I am Madhu. That was the whole reasoning. Akhilu then did the actual work and narrowed it down to two options — Mukund or Milind. I picked Mukund. I would like to formally take credit for that 50/50 coin flip in front of all of you, because I am fairly certain I do not get credit for many other things in this marriage.

AKHILA

I'll give you that one, Chinnu.

MADHU

Thank you, Chinnu.

The name Mukund means "the one who grants liberation." In the original story, Lord Krishna's birth was a midnight cosmic drama — storm, serpent, the river itself parting to let his father carry him through.

Our version was less theatrical. Just one very small baby with a giant head — taken after me, naturally — announcing himself to the world at the top of his lungs, as if to say, "Excuse me, why did you bring me out here? I was perfectly happy inside Amma. Send me back, please."

But he has spent every year since living up to the liberation part of the name — mostly by trying to liberate himself from us.

AKHILA

Even as a little boy, Mukund stood out for his curiosity, his resilience, and his patience. The very first toy he reached for was a Lego block — which, honestly, explained a lot. He loved building things, organizing things, fixing things. A real-life Bob the Builder with a sparkle in his eyes.

MADHU

And Chinnu — you remember what he did at the state Lego championship.

AKHILA

Oh, that I remember.

MADHU

Mukund led his FLL Lego team to win the Oregon state championship — beating 120 other teams. And he did it by redesigning their robot from scratch with a four-wheel drive design no team had attempted before.

AKHILA

That was the moment we realized he was not just playing with Legos. He was engineering.

AKHILA

And he was independent from day one. Madhu had it especially bad —

MADHU

Tell them, Chinnu.

AKHILA

We had a little bike with training wheels, and the moment Mukund realized Madhu was not holding on anymore, he just dropped the bike and ran. No goodbye. No thank you. Just freedom.

But that same fiercely independent little boy, on his very first day of preschool, cried so hard and held on to me so tightly that the teacher had to carry him on her lap for the entire class. That is our Mukund — fiercely independent, and deeply attached to the people he loves.

And from the day Medini was born four years later, the two of them were inseparable. Best friends, teammates, and occasionally partners in crime. They still are.

AKHILA

There were many funny stories along the way.

MADHU

Many.

AKHILA

There was the time he ate an entire container of sweet homeopathy pills, and when we asked what happened, he innocently said, "Amma, I didn't do anything. I just swallowed all of them."

MADHU

And there was the shaving incident in Bangalore. Mukund was about 7. He used to love watching me shave —

AKHILA

One morning, we heard crying from the bathroom. We rushed in. He was standing there with Madhu's razor, with a cut on his lip. And he looked up at us, with the most innocent face, and said —

BOTH

"A mosquito bit me!"

AKHILA

And then there was the Game Boy. He came to me holding it. "Amma, it is not working." I asked what happened. He said, "It felt dirty. So I put water on it to clean it."

MADHU

That was the end of the Game Boy. But the start of a beautiful engineering career.

AKHILA

Take it from here, Chinnu.

MADHU

Happily. The boy who once washed his own Game Boy to clean it now works as a software engineer at Meta — Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, the entire empire. The same kid who short-circuited his own Game Boy is now writing code that runs on three billion phones. Okay — slight exaggeration. But you get the idea.

Now — to be fair — we are also a family that always, always eats dinner together. That is Akhilu. She has held that line for years. We even had a stretch where every night at the table, the four of us would each share the highlight of our day and the lowlight of our day. That part — I would like to point out — was my idea.

AKHILA

I'll give you that one too, Chinnu.

MADHU

Thank you. So between Akhilu's family-dinner rule and my highlight-lowlight protocol, you would think we had it covered. But now our son works at Meta — and he is professionally undermining all of it, one notification at a time.

AKHILA

Every notification.

MADHU

And speaking of Divya for a moment — let us appreciate this combination. Divya is a doctor. She literally saves lives. Mukund is an engineer at Meta. He literally keeps people on their phones for hours. Divya treats the patients. Mukund builds the apps that send them there. It is the most beautifully balanced marriage in modern medicine.

MADHU

Mukund is also a perfect demonstration of how genetics work. He got Akhilu's eyes — beautiful. He got her cleanliness, her meticulousness, her fastidious nature. From me, he inherited my big fat nose and my terrible short-term memory. So Akhila clearly won that round. My one contribution to this entire project remains: the letter M.

MADHU

Our family has always been an outdoors family. One of the best parts of living in Portland is the mountains and the trails. Akhilu and I have lost count of the hikes and the trips the four of us have done together over the years.

But there is one day I will never forget. Mukund — when you, Medini, and I scaled Mount St. Helens together. We trained for months — every brutal hike we could find around Oregon and Washington. And on the day, the three of us made it to the top together. I will carry that day with me forever. And buddy — we still have Mount Adams on the list.

Now here is the funny part. When you were younger, you hated running. Absolutely hated it. Basketball, tennis, anything with a ball — fine. But running? Forget it. And yet — just recently — you ran a half marathon. Thirteen point one miles. So Mukund — I am formally claiming this one. Amma gave you the eyes, the patience, the meticulousness, and ninety percent of the rest. But the endurance? The running? That, my son, is from me. Add it to the letter M.

AKHILA

Some memories stay with a mother forever. I will always remember the night Mukund called me at 11 PM, when he was a teenager preparing for a speech competition after a difficult day. He simply said, "Amma, I just wanted to talk." And then there were our quiet walks together in the beautiful Pacific Northwest, just the two of us — moments I will treasure for the rest of my life.

From Portland to Chicago to Seattle, Mukund has carried himself with quiet strength and grace through every transition. Watching him face those harsh Chicago winters thousands of miles away was not easy. But he built friendships, he thrived, and he has grown into the thoughtful, grounded man we are so proud of today. And through all of it — he has remained a devoted foodie. Nothing has made me happier than sending him his favorite meals.

AKHILA

Divya — today we welcome you with open hearts into our family. Into our circle of warmth, love, laughter, and deep care for one another. You bring so much joy to Mukund's life, and it is easy to see why he cherishes you so deeply.

Mukund and Divya — marriage will bring beautiful seasons and difficult ones. It is not about chasing perfection. It is about kindness. Patience. Forgiveness. Respect. And choosing each other — over and over again.

May you always remain each other's safest place.

MADHU

Before we close, Akhila and I want to thank a few very important people who have made this day what it is.

AKHILA

First, to my beloved sister Anitha — I would be lost without you. Thank you for being my rock, for traveling all the way from Bangalore, and for that beautiful song.

To my lovely sister-in-law Veena — I appreciate you more than words can ever express.

To my lifelong anchors Arathi and Malini; our dear friends Vasanthi and Vasu in Bengaluru; Padmashree and Seshu here in Portland; HariLakshmi, Ruchi, and Shanmu; and to Madhu's cousins Savitha, Kavitha, and Soumya — your love and support have meant the world to us.

MADHU

And from my side — to Ravi, my brother-in-law — thank you for keeping things light, and for the one time you got me completely drunk. We will discuss the details privately.

To Rhea, my favorite niece and a sharp software engineer; Chintu, my favorite nephew and a budding entrepreneur; and my cousins Smitha and Ramya and their kids — thank you for making the trip. Chintu, you are going to do wonders.

We missed Venkatesh, Vikram, Vardhan, Srinidhi, and Jyotsna today. And Mythreyi, we hope your surgery went well — sending love your way.

To Omesh, my friend from the CG CoreEL days in Pune, here today with your son — thank you for coming.

And to Venkatesh, my closest cousin — more like a brother than a cousin. He could not be here due to visa restrictions. Venkatesh — you are missed today more than anyone.

BOTH

To all of our family back in India, and to all of our friends here in Portland who are too many to name — thank you for being part of our lives. Your blessings and love have carried us here.

AKHILA

And to my beloved daughter Medini — you warm my heart every day by always being there for me, without judgment. Thank you, kanna.

To my beloved husband of thirty years, Madhu — thank you for being my best and forever friend.

MADHU

Akhilu — I have spent this entire speech taking credit for the letter M and giving you credit for everything else, and honestly, that math is about right. You gave Mukund your eyes, your patience, your meticulousness, your discipline, and your kindness. You raised these two extraordinary kids. You have put up with my big fat nose and my terrible short-term memory for thirty years — and somehow you are still here, smiling, next to me.

Mukund and Divya — exactly thirty years ago, Akhilu and I started our own version of what you are starting today. If your marriage is half as good as the one she has built with me, you are going to be very, very lucky.

Akhilu — I love you.

AKHILA

Today is not only the union of Mukund and Divya, but the coming together of two families and two traditions — Kannada and Tamil — joined by shared values of love, faith, generosity, and community.

Unwavering faith in God has always been the bedrock of our lives. And as you begin this new chapter together, we pray that same faith continues to guide and strengthen you both. May you always know that a higher power walks beside you.

May your home be filled with peace, health, prosperity, and love. And may your life together always be rich in laughter, shared dreams, and deep companionship.

MADHU

Mukund — we love you. Divya — welcome to the chaos. You got the eyes, the meticulousness, the engineer's brain, and a husband who will figure out how to fix the car before he tells you it is broken. You are going to be just fine.

BOTH

To Mukund and Divya — cheers.
