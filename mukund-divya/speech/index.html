A Father’s Speech

From the desk of Madhu

Good evening, everyone. For those who don’t know me, I’m Madhu — Mukund’s dad. And tonight, after 26 years of trying, Akhila and I finally get to embarrass him in front of every single person he knows. Thank you all for making that possible.

Let me start with some numbers, because Mukund has been a numbers guy since the very beginning. He was born on May 1st, 2000, at 1:23 AM. One, two, three. The boy showed up in numerical order. He’s been precise about everything since.

Akhila and I named him Mukund — one of the names of Lord Krishna. Small piece of family history we want to put on the record tonight: my entire contribution to naming our firstborn child was the requirement that his name start with M. Why M? Because I am Madhu. M for Madhu. That was the whole reasoning. My full creative input as a father was to make sure my son shared the first letter of my own name. Akhila then did all of the actual work and narrowed it down to two options — Mukund or Milind. I picked Mukund. I would like to formally take credit for that 50/50 coin flip in front of all of you, because I am fairly certain I do not get credit for many other things in this marriage. The name means “the one who grants liberation.” Now, in the original story, Lord Krishna was born at midnight in the middle of a massive storm — thunder, lightning, his father carrying him across a flooded river, a giant cosmic serpent holding its hood over the basket like an umbrella, the river itself parting to let them through. Very dramatic. Very cinematic.

Our version was a little less theatrical. No storm. No snake. No river parting. Just one very small baby with a giant head — taken after me again, naturally — arriving and immediately announcing himself to the world at the top of his lungs, as if to say, “Excuse me — why did you bring me out here? I was perfectly happy inside Amma. It was warm. It was quiet. Send me back, please.” But he has spent every year since living up to the liberation part of the name — mostly by trying to liberate himself from us.

By 11 months, he was already walking and talking, and most of what he was doing was running away from us. We had a little bike with training wheels. We’d take him out to teach him how to ride. He’d pedal for two minutes, look back, see I was a few feet behind him — and just drop the bike and dart. No goodbye. No thank you. Just freedom.

And yet — this same fiercely independent child, when we dropped him at his very first Baby and Me class, refused to let Akhila out of his sight. He cried so much the teacher ended up holding him on her lap for the entire class. So that’s our son: fully independent, as long as Amma is in the room.

He was an engineer from day one. Curious about everything. And by “hands on,” I mean if his tiny fingers found anything on the floor, it went straight into his mouth. That’s a different kind of hands-on than what got him into Meta, but the spirit was the same.

He loved Bob the Builder. He had the hat, the costume, the tools — he would disappear for hours and come back having built something. Give him Legos and you would not see him until dinner.

Now — a word about his curiosity, which has occasionally been expensive.

When Mukund was about 7, we were visiting Bangalore. He used to love watching me shave. One morning, I heard crying from the bathroom. I walked in and found my son standing there with my razor, blood running down both cheeks. He looked at me and said, “Appa, you make it look so clean. How come it doesn’t bleed for you?” That was my first hint that this boy was going to take things apart to figure out how they work.

Around the same time, he came to me holding his Game Boy. “Appa, it’s not working.” I asked what happened. He said, “It felt dirty. So I put water on it to clean it.” That was the end of the Game Boy. But the start of a beautiful engineering career.

And what a career it has been. The boy who once tried to clean his Game Boy with tap water now works as a software engineer at Meta. Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Threads — the entire empire. So the same kid who short-circuited his own Game Boy is now writing code that runs on three billion phones. We want everyone to take a moment to appreciate how far technology has come.

Having an engineer at Meta in the family has its perks. We get free tech support — eventually, whenever he picks up. And it has its costs. Akhila has a strict “no phones at dinner” policy. Mukund is professionally undermining it. Every notification on my phone, every ad in my Instagram feed, every reel I cannot stop watching — somewhere in the chain, my own son is responsible.

His ultimate boss, of course, is a man who has worn the same gray t-shirt every day for fifteen years. Mukund — buddy — please do not bring that home. Divya did not sign up for that.

And speaking of Divya — let us appreciate this combination for a moment. Divya is a doctor. She literally saves lives. Mukund is an engineer at Meta. He literally keeps people on their phones for hours. Divya treats the patients. Mukund builds the apps that send them there. It is the most beautifully balanced marriage in modern medicine.

And to be fair, we should have seen it coming. We named the boy after Lord Krishna — the original cosmic strategist. Of course he ended up running the algorithm.

· · ·

Mukund is also a perfect demonstration of how genetics work. He got Akhila’s eyes — beautiful. He got her cleanliness, her meticulousness, her fastidious nature. From me, he inherited my big fat nose and my terrible short-term memory. So Akhila clearly won that round. My one contribution to this entire project remains: the letter M.

His cousin Rhea calls him “Mr. Strictman” — because he keeps his emotions tucked up his sleeve and likes to handle everything himself. Two stories.

Years ago, we were on a road trip, and our Audi started flashing the low oil warning in the middle of nowhere. I’m fumbling with the engine trying to figure out where the oil even goes — Audis have a strange way of doing this — and before I could finish the sentence, Mukund had already figured it out. He stood there politely, while I pretended I had it under control.

Another time, in high school, he was backing the Acura out of a friend’s driveway and hit a parked car across the street. Real damage. Most kids would call their dad in tears. Not Mukund. He spent the next few hours researching repair shops, estimates, the whole plan. Then he called me and said, “Appa, this happened — but I’ve already figured everything out for you.” Mukund — buddy — sometimes it’s okay to call Appa first and then figure things out.

A quick word about his first love. Basketball. When he was little, someone asked him what he wanted to be when he grew up. “A basketball player.” That was the dream. Unfortunately, our family contributed many things to Mukund — but height was not one of them. So he pivoted to tennis, made the team, and the rest is history. Sometimes life redesigns your robot for you.

· · ·

But here is the thing about our family — we have always been an outdoors family. One of the best parts of living in Portland is the mountains, the trails, the long weekend hikes. Akhila and I have lost count of the trips, the hikes, the adventures the four of us have done together over the years.

But there is one day I will never forget. Mukund — when you, Medini, and I scaled Mount St. Helens. That was one of the proudest days of my life. We trained for months. We did Mount Defiance. We did every brutal hike we could find around Oregon and Washington. We built ourselves up to it. And on the day, the three of us made it to the top together. I will carry that day with me forever. And buddy — we still have Mount Adams on the list. I have not forgotten.

Now here is the funny part. When you were younger, you hated running. Absolutely hated it. Basketball, tennis, anything with a ball — fine. But running? Forget it.

And yet — just recently — you went and ran a half marathon. Thirteen point one miles. So Mukund — I am formally claiming this one. Yes, Amma gave you the eyes, the patience, the meticulousness, and ninety percent of the rest. But the endurance? The running? The half marathon? That, my son, is from me. Add it to the letter M.

First, to Medini — Mukund’s sister, our daughter. From day one she has been Mukund’s closest friend, his confidant, and his co-conspirator. The two of them have been plotting against Akhila and me, swapping secrets, and hiding things from us for as long as I can remember. Jokes aside, Medini is the voice of reason in our family — which, of course, she gets from me. I am claiming full credit for that one too. Thank you, Medini, for being everything you are to your brother.

To Anithu — Akhila’s sister, my sister-in-law — thank you for your love and support, for always being there for Mukund, and for making the trip all the way from Bangalore. And Anithu, we loved your song.

To Veena, my sister — thank you for reminding us every day what love and support look like. Not just for me, but for Akhila too. We love you.

To Ravi, my brother-in-law — thank you for always being there, for cracking the joke when one is needed, and for keeping things light. I have not forgotten the time you got me completely drunk. We will discuss the details privately. Thank you, Ravi.

To Rhea — my favorite niece, and a sharp software engineer in her own right — thank you for making it here with everything you have going on between work and travel. We are so glad you came.

To Chintu — my favorite nephew and a budding entrepreneur — you are going to do wonders. We cannot wait to watch.

To my cousins Smitha and Ramya, who came with their kids — thank you for being here. We missed Venkatesh, Vikram, and Vardhan today and would have loved to have them with us as well.

To Srinidhi and Jyotsna — we missed you. Sorry the circumstances did not allow you to make it.

To Mythreyi — we missed you too. We hope your surgery went well, and we are sending love your way.

To all of our uncles and aunts back in India, and to all of Akhila’s cousins, uncles, and aunts in India — thank you for your blessings and your well wishes. We feel them across the ocean.

To Omesh — my friend from the CG CoreEL days, here today with your son — thank you for coming. I cannot forget the days we spent together in Pune.

To all of my close friends in India who have sent their wishes — thank you. You are with us in spirit.

And last but absolutely not least — Venkatesh, my closest cousin, who is more like a brother to me than a cousin. He is there for me and for our family for everything. He could not be here today due to visa restrictions. Venkatesh — you are missed today more than anyone. We love you.

And finally — to all of our Portland friends and family who are here, there are too many of you to name individually, but thank you for being a part of our lives. We could not have done any of this without you.

And finally — before we raise a glass — I have to thank the most important person in this room for me. Akhila. My wife, my partner, and my best friend for thirty years.

I have spent this entire speech taking credit for the letter M and giving you credit for everything else, and honestly, that math is about right. You gave Mukund your eyes, your patience, your meticulousness, your discipline, and your kindness. You built this family. You raised these two extraordinary kids. You have put up with my big fat nose and my terrible short-term memory for three decades — and somehow you are still here, smiling, next to me.

Mukund and Divya — exactly thirty years ago, Akhila and I started our own version of what you are starting today. If your marriage is half as good as the one Akhila has built with me, you are going to be very, very lucky.

Akhila — thank you. I love you.

Now — Mukund — we love you. Divya — welcome to the chaos. You got the eyes, the meticulousness, the engineer’s brain, and a husband who will figure out how to fix the car before he tells you it is broken. You are going to be just fine.

To Mukund and Divya — cheers.
